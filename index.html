    What is a Carbon Footprint?

    A carbon footprint represents the total greenhouse gas (GHG) emissions generated directly and indirectly by our actions. It is measured in kilograms of carbon dioxide equivalent (kg CO2e), which accounts for CO2 alongside other potent warming gases like methane and nitrous oxide.

    Every sector of daily life—from the fuel burned in cars and the electric grids that light our homes, to the agriculture that feeds us and the manufacturing lines that produce our appliances—contributes to this total. Tracking these categories highlights where simple shifts can yield major improvements.

    Understanding Category Impacts

    Transportation:
    Burning oil-based fuels generates tailpipe gases. Aviation has high radiative forcing effects high in the atmosphere, multiplying flight impact.
    Home Energy:
    Grid electricity often depends on coal or natural gas combustion. Efficient appliances, solar cells, and smart cooling reduce home grid dependency.
    Food & Diet:
    Livestock farming (especially beef) creates high methane (CH4) emissions. Local sourcing reduces freight logistics impacts.
    Shopping & Waste:
    Industrial manufacturing processes consume vast fossil energies. Food waste decomposition in landfill environments releases methane, avoidable via composting.

    Frequently Asked Questions

    How accurate are these carbon estimates?
    These metrics are dynamic estimates calculated using standardized average factors (e.g. EPA and Defra parameters). They are designed for educational awareness, highlighting major trends rather than representing a strict scientific audit.
    What is a carbon target / budget?
    A target budget represents the maximum weekly emissions you aim to stay under. Setting a personal budget lets you track compliance and measure your incremental reductions. A standard starter goal is 60 kg CO2e per week.
    How do selected Action Plan tasks affect calculations?
    Checking items on your Action Plan marks them as active goals. The dashboard displays the potential weekly carbon savings from your active choices, highlighting your progress toward carbon neutrality.
    Where can I find my home electricity metrics?
    Check your monthly utility bill for a consumption number in kilowatt-hours (kWh). If you share a household, divide the bill total by the number of occupants to find your personal share.
